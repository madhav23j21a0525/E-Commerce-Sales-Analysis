# Business Insights Report — E-Commerce Sales Analysis
**Dataset:** Olist Brazilian E-Commerce (Sep 2016 – Aug 2018, delivered orders only)
**Total Revenue Analysed:** R$ 15,419,773.75 | **Total Orders:** 96,478

---

### 1. Health & Beauty leads revenue, but the top 3 categories are nearly tied
*(Reference: Chart 1 — Revenue by Product Category)*

Health & Beauty is the top-earning category at R$1.41M, but it's barely ahead of Watches & Gifts (R$1.26M) and Bed & Bath (R$1.23M) — together the top 3 make up just 25.3% of total revenue, meaning no single category dominates the catalog.

**Recommendation:** Don't over-concentrate marketing spend on Health & Beauty alone. Run comparable promotional pushes across all three leading categories, and track weekly whether the ranking holds — a lead this narrow can flip with one good campaign from a competitor category.

---

### 2. Sales peak sharply in November, then drop just as fast
*(Reference: Chart 2 — Monthly Revenue Trend)*

November 2017 was the single best month (R$1.15M), up 53.6% from October — almost certainly a Black Friday effect. Revenue then fell 26.9% in December, the steepest month-over-month drop anywhere in the trend.

**Recommendation:** Treat November as a planned demand spike, not an organic one — pre-book inventory and seller capacity ahead of it. More importantly, build a specific December retention campaign (targeted at November's new buyers) to soften the post-Black-Friday cliff instead of letting it fall on its own.

---

### 3. Revenue is heavily concentrated in São Paulo, leaving most of the country underserved
*(Reference: Chart 3 — Revenue by State)*

São Paulo alone generates 37.4% of all revenue (R$5.77M), more than the next four states combined. The 10 lowest-revenue states together contribute only R$560K — about 3.6% of total revenue.

**Recommendation:** Before investing further in already-saturated SP demand, test targeted regional marketing and delivery-fee subsidies in the next tier of states (RJ, MG, RS, PR), which show real but under-realized volume. There's more incremental revenue available from expanding reach than from squeezing an already-dominant state further.

---

### 4. Late deliveries are the single biggest driver of bad reviews
*(Reference: Chart 5 — Review Score Distribution, cross-checked against delivery dates)*

Orders delivered after the estimated delivery date average a review score of just 2.57/5, versus 4.30/5 for on-time orders — a gap of 1.73 points. Only 8.0% of delivered orders arrive late, but they are disproportionately responsible for the 12.75% of orders rating 1–2 stars shown in the pie chart.

**Recommendation:** This is the highest-leverage fix available: even modest improvement in on-time delivery rate (via carrier SLAs or safer estimated-delivery buffers) will move the review distribution more than any other single lever, since it isolates a small, identifiable group of orders causing an outsized share of dissatisfaction.

---

### 5. Average order value has drifted down even as order volume exploded
*(Reference: Chart 4 — Order Value Distribution, and the AOV trend underlying Chart 2)*

Monthly order volume grew from 265 orders (first full month) to 6,351 orders (last full month) — a 24x increase — but average order value actually declined slightly over the same period, from R$175.44 to R$155.17.

**Recommendation:** Growth so far has come almost entirely from acquiring more orders, not from increasing basket size. Introduce cross-sell/bundle prompts at checkout (e.g. "customers who bought this also bought…") and free-shipping thresholds set just above the current AOV to nudge customers toward larger baskets — this converts existing traffic into more revenue without needing to acquire even more orders.

---

## Most Surprising Finding

The most surprising result wasn't which category or region led — it was how small the "late delivery" segment is (8%) relative to how much damage it does to review scores (a 1.73-point average swing). It suggests review-score problems aren't a broad quality issue across the business, but a narrow, fixable logistics problem. Fixing on-time delivery rate would likely do more for customer satisfaction than any product or pricing change.
